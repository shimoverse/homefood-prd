Neighborhood Home Food Discovery

A WhatsApp-first, web-backed marketplace for discovering who is cooking nearby, what is available today, whether it fits your diet, how far it is, what it costs, and whether it is still available.

The Problem

Food discovery is trapped inside noisy local channels.

The current behavior is real: home cooks post daily menus in WhatsApp, Facebook, Nextdoor, temple groups, school groups, and private friend networks. Buyers want the food, but they do not want the monitoring burden.

Buyer pain

  • Menus are scattered across many groups and chats.
  • Availability changes quickly; good items sell out.
  • Dietary filters are manual: Jain, vegan, eggless, gluten-free, nut-free, spice level.
  • Distance, pickup windows, price, and order method are not normalized.
  • Newcomers do not know which groups or cooks exist.

Seller pain

  • Small food sellers rely on repeated self-promotion.
  • They cannot easily reach adjacent neighborhoods or new residents.
  • They manually handle DMs, counts, substitutions, pickup reminders, and sold-out updates.
  • Most cannot justify a full app, website, or delivery operation.
  • Trust depends on word-of-mouth instead of portable reputation.

Sharper thesis

The product should not start as a restaurant marketplace. It should start as a trusted availability radar: structured daily supply from known local cooks, personalized by distance, diet, pickup window, and availability.

Personas

The product has to serve both sides without making either side change too much.

The winning product is not another social feed. It is an availability layer over local food supply.

BF

Busy Family Buyer

I need dinner options within 15 minutes that my family can actually eat.

  • Filters by cuisine, vegetarian/Jain/eggless, pickup time, distance.
  • Wants fast trust signals: known community, ratings, repeat buyers.
  • Needs sold-out status before messaging the seller.
  • Job to be done: replace dinner decision fatigue with a short trusted shortlist.
DP

Dietary Preference Buyer

I do not want to ask every cook whether today's menu fits my constraints.

  • Jain, vegan, no onion/garlic, nut-free, gluten-free, low spice.
  • Needs confidence in ingredient disclosure.
  • Will subscribe to alerts for matching drops.
  • Job to be done: avoid repetitive ingredient interrogation.
NR

New Resident

I just moved here. I do not know the WhatsApp groups or who cooks well.

  • Needs local onboarding: area, language, cuisine, family size.
  • Values discovery more than discounts.
  • Trusts community proof and referrals.
  • Job to be done: find the hidden neighborhood food network quickly.
HC

Home Cook Seller

I cook well, but I do not want to become a marketer or support desk.

  • Needs a 2-minute way to post today's menu.
  • Needs caps, waitlist, pickup instructions, and repeat buyer list.
  • May start informal but needs a path to compliance.
  • Job to be done: sell out without becoming a full-time marketer.
SP

Specialty Pop-up Seller

For biryani, sweets, cakes, or festival food, people will drive farther.

  • Needs radius expansion for special items.
  • Benefits from pre-order drops and scarcity.
  • Wants social sharing and sold-out proof.
  • Job to be done: create demand before cooking inventory.
GA

Group Admin / Community Connector

I want useful food posts without my group turning into spam.

  • Needs opt-in digests and clean summaries.
  • Can seed trust and seller discovery.
  • May become the local ambassador.
  • Job to be done: keep community utility high and spam low.

Market Scan

Existing solutions prove demand, but most solve the wrong layer for this use case.

The market splits into chef storefront tools, full marketplaces, and neighborhood/social channels. Your opportunity is the thin local discovery layer connecting fragmented supply to intent.

What to copy

  • Drop mechanics from seller tools: schedule, scarcity, sell-out proof.
  • Profile trust from marketplaces: photos, repeat buyers, ratings, badges.
  • Low-friction alerts from messaging: opt-in digest, not another feed.

What to avoid

  • Starting with payments, delivery, refunds, and support tickets.
  • Building a native app before proving daily/weekly retention.
  • Assuming public listings alone solve trust in prepared food.

Unfair wedge

  • Dietary precision: Jain, no onion/garlic, eggless, vegan, allergy flags.
  • Neighborhood density: Tracy and adjacent Bay Area communities first.
  • Community graph: known groups, temples, schools, referrals, admins.

Recommendation

Start WhatsApp-first, not app-first.

A native app is the wrong first move. The behavior already lives in messaging. The MVP should aggregate and structure daily food drops, then give buyers a clean web view and personalized WhatsApp/SMS alerts.

Buyer surface

A mobile web page: "What's cooking near me today?" with filters for location, diet, cuisine, pickup time, price, and sold-out status.

Seller surface

A WhatsApp/SMS bot or simple form: post menu, price, quantity, pickup window, dietary tags, photo, and location radius.

Discovery surface

Daily personalized digest: "5 Jain/veg options within 8 miles today" plus urgent sold-out and new-seller alerts.

Positioning

"The local food radar for home-cooked meals, tiffins, festival specials, and neighborhood food drops."

Do not position as "Uber Eats for home cooks" initially. That invites regulatory, delivery, and marketplace expectations too early.

MVP Decision Scorecard

Must have

Seller opt-in, today's menu, distance, diet tags, price, pickup window, availability, contact path.

Should have

Digest personalization, sold-out toggle, seller profile, share card, admin moderation, repeat buyer signals.

Later

Payments, delivery, app, loyalty, subscriptions, catering marketplace, automated OCR ingestion.

Hard no

No anonymous prepared-food sellers, no private scraping, no delivery promise, no implied licensing guarantee.

PRD

Product Requirements Document

Working title: LocalPlate Radar. Goal: reduce daily discovery time from 15-30 minutes across chats to under 60 seconds.

Objective

  • Make local home-food availability searchable and personalized.
  • Give sellers lightweight demand capture without forcing them into a full storefront.
  • Build trust and compliance progressively, not as a blocker to discovery.

Non-goals for MVP

  • No delivery fleet.
  • No full payment marketplace at launch.
  • No scraping private WhatsApp groups without explicit seller/admin opt-in.
  • No anonymous sellers for prepared meals.

MVP Phases

Phase 0 - Concierge Validation

Pick one geography and one community. Manually onboard 15 sellers and 100 buyers. Publish a daily WhatsApp digest and a simple web directory. Measure opens, clicks, messages, and sell-outs.

Phase 1 - Structured Drops

Sellers post via form or WhatsApp bot. Buyers browse a web catalog and subscribe to dietary/location alerts. Inventory status is manual but structured: available, few left, sold out.

Phase 2 - Pre-order + Waitlist

Add reservations, quantity caps, pickup windows, cancellation rules, and waitlist. Keep payment optional until compliance and dispute flows are clear.

Phase 3 - Trust + Compliance

Add seller verification, allergen disclosure, kitchen license/compliance badges where applicable, reviews, incident reporting, and repeat-buyer signals.

Phase 4 - Marketplace Expansion

Payments, delivery partners, premium seller tools, sponsored placement, festival bundles, catering requests, and cross-neighborhood discovery.

Acceptance Criteria

Buyer

  • Can see today's nearby options without joining any group.
  • Can filter by diet, distance, cuisine, pickup time, and availability.
  • Can contact or reserve from a listing in one tap.

Seller

  • Can publish a drop from phone in under 2 minutes.
  • Can mark few-left or sold-out without admin help.
  • Can reuse yesterday's menu and edit price/quantity.

Admin

  • Can approve sellers, correct listings, handle reports, and export demand data.
  • Can pause a seller if trust or compliance issues emerge.
  • Can send one clean digest per neighborhood.

Operating Rules

Trust and safety

  • Display compliance status as disclosed/verified, not assumed.
  • Require allergen and dietary disclaimers for every drop.
  • Use report flow for illness, misrepresentation, no-show, and payment disputes.
  • Keep seller contact visible only after buyer intent to reduce spam.

Regulatory posture

  • Discovery-first MVP; do not process payments until local rules are reviewed.
  • Separate cottage-food eligible items from prepared hot meals where required.
  • Badge sellers by disclosed permits, verified permits, or community-only status.
  • Never market as food-safety certified unless there is explicit verification.

Metrics

Measure liquidity and repeat behavior, not vanity signups.

This idea only works if buyers repeatedly check it and sellers repeatedly update supply. Everything else is secondary.

Activation

  • Buyer saves location + diet preferences.
  • Seller publishes first drop.
  • Buyer clicks/message/reserves within 7 days.

Liquidity

  • Active sellers per day.
  • Listings per cuisine/diet/neighborhood.
  • Percent of listings updated before 4 PM.

Retention

  • Digest open 3+ days/week.
  • Buyer repeat intent within 14 days.
  • Seller posts 2+ consecutive weeks.

Quality

  • Sold-out accuracy.
  • Report rate per 100 intents.
  • Diet/allergen mismatch rate.

North star

Weekly matched food intent: the number of buyer actions on listings that match location, diet, and availability. This keeps the team focused on useful matches rather than catalog size.

Distribution

Win one neighborhood and one cuisine community before building broad marketplace machinery.

The first distribution goal is not app installs. It is daily habit formation around "what is cooking today?"

Supply acquisition

  • Start with 20-30 sellers from WhatsApp/Facebook/temple/school referrals.
  • Offer free landing page + weekly demand report.
  • Give sellers shareable "today's drop" cards.
  • Recruit one community ambassador per city/neighborhood.

Demand acquisition

  • WhatsApp community digest by neighborhood.
  • Nextdoor posts focused on discovery, not aggressive selling.
  • Facebook group posts: "today's home-cooked options near Tracy".
  • Temple/community center flyers with QR codes.

Retention loops

  • Personalized daily alert at 4 PM.
  • Friday/weekend special drops.
  • "Sold out in 42 minutes" proof for sellers.
  • Buyer preference memory: no onion/garlic, Jain, vegan, kid-friendly.

Channel decision

Use WhatsApp/SMS + web first. A native app should come only after there is repeated weekly usage and enough supply density. WhatsApp is the input and notification layer; web is the browsing and SEO layer; app is a later retention layer.

Launch Playbook

Community wedge

Start with Tracy / Bay Area Indian, Jain, vegetarian, family, school, and temple networks where dietary specificity matters.

Seller loop

Give sellers share cards, sold-out screenshots, and a weekly demand report so they invite other sellers organically.

Buyer loop

Make each listing shareable: "look what is cooking today" should bring buyers back to the web catalog, not only to a chat.

Admin loop

Recruit trusted group admins as local curators. Their reward can be visibility, free featured placements, or neighborhood ambassador credit.

Devil's Advocate

The idea is useful, but the hard parts are not UI.

The risk is operational trust, not whether people want homemade food.

Reasons it could fail

  • Local density is hard: buyers churn if there are not enough options nearby.
  • Prepared food regulation varies by county/state and can limit what sellers can legally sell.
  • Food safety incidents can damage trust quickly.
  • Home cooks may not want another tool; they may prefer WhatsApp only.
  • Buyers may browse but still order through personal relationships.
  • Delivery expectations can inflate costs and complexity.
  • Supply may be too inconsistent for a daily habit unless sellers get repeated demand.
  • Group admins may see this as extracting value from their communities.

How to de-risk

  • Start as discovery + lead-gen, not payment marketplace.
  • Require seller opt-in and structured posting.
  • Use compliance badges instead of claiming all sellers are licensed.
  • Focus first on pre-order drops and pickup, not on-demand delivery.
  • Pick one high-trust community and make it excellent.
  • Measure repeat buyer behavior before adding app complexity.
  • Make admins partners, not bypassed gatekeepers.
  • Publish clear rules for prepared meals, allergens, pickup, refunds, and reporting.

Fatal risks

  • Compliance path is too restrictive for the chosen category.
  • Trust incident before reputation mechanics exist.
  • Cannot reach seller density in a single neighborhood.

Manageable risks

  • Seller onboarding takes handholding.
  • Menus are inconsistent or posted late.
  • Buyers prefer messaging over in-product checkout.

Kill criteria

  • Fewer than 20 opt-in sellers after 30 days of direct outreach.
  • Fewer than 25% of buyers return weekly after two digest cycles.
  • Fewer than 10% of sellers post again after their first listing.
  • Legal/compliance ambiguity prevents responsible operation.

Next Steps

30-day day-by-day validation plan

Replace vague weekly milestones with daily actions, measurable outputs, and clear decision gates.

Day 1

Pick the wedge

Choose one launch area and one core community, for example Tracy Indian/Jain/vegetarian families.

Day 2

Define taxonomy

Lock cuisine, diet, allergen, pickup, radius, and availability tags. Keep the first version under 25 tags.

Day 3

Interview buyers

Talk to 10 buyers. Capture current groups used, last order, dietary filters, willingness to receive a digest.

Day 4

Interview sellers

Talk to 10 sellers. Capture posting workflow, capacity, pricing, pickup rules, trust concerns, and compliance status.

Day 5

Compliance scan

Document local food-sale constraints, cottage-food boundaries, disclosure language, and categories to avoid at MVP.

Day 6

Build intake

Create seller intake form and buyer preference form. Include opt-in language and no-private-scraping policy.

Day 7

Recruit first sellers

Secure 10 opt-in sellers and collect two sample drops each. Confirm photos, pickup areas, and contact preferences.

Day 8

Create digest template

Format one daily digest with menu, diet tags, price, distance, pickup window, few-left/sold-out status.

Day 9

Launch buyer waitlist

Invite 50-100 buyers from trusted channels. Do not overpromise delivery, payment, or coverage.

Day 10

Send first digest

Manually send the first WhatsApp/SMS/email-free digest. Track views, clicks, replies, and seller messages.

Day 11

Measure friction

Ask buyers what was missing before they could decide. Ask sellers what was annoying about updating availability.

Day 12

Add availability status

Introduce available, few left, sold out, preorder closed. Make it visible in the digest and catalog.

Day 13

Add dietary matching

Send a personalized digest variant for Jain/veg/no onion-garlic/vegan buyers.

Day 14

Seller feedback loop

Send sellers a simple demand report: views, messages, top filters, and whether they sold out.

Day 15

Launch web catalog v0

Publish a mobile web catalog with today's listings, filters, and contact/reserve intent. Keep backend manual if needed.

Day 16

QR + share cards

Create seller-specific share cards and QR flyers for temples, schools, apartment buildings, and community centers.

Day 17

Community posts

Post in approved Facebook/Nextdoor/community channels with admin consent. Frame it as a useful local directory.

Day 18

Preference memory

Let buyers save radius, diet, cuisine, and notification time. Use these preferences in the next digest.

Day 19

Waitlist / reserve intent

Add a lightweight reserve or waitlist action for drops with limited quantity. Keep payment offline.

Day 20

Seller self-posting

Let sellers post or duplicate a drop through form or WhatsApp bot. Admin reviews before publishing.

Day 21

Density review

Check whether each buyer segment sees at least 3 relevant options per week. If not, narrow or change wedge.

Day 22

Trust layer

Add seller profile, disclosed compliance status, repeat buyer count, rating prompt, and report link.

Day 23

Special drop lane

Test sweets, biryani, festival foods, cakes, and catering as higher-radius listings.

Day 24

Radius experiment

Compare normal meals within 5-10 miles versus specialty items up to 50 miles. Measure intent, not opinions.

Day 25

Operations SOP

Write the repeatable daily process: seller reminder, listing review, digest send, sold-out updates, incident handling.

Day 26

Pricing signal

Test seller willingness to pay for featured drops, demand reports, repeat buyer list, or premium listing tools.

Day 27

Churn survey

Ask inactive buyers and sellers why they stopped. Categorize into density, trust, timing, price, and friction.

Day 28

Safety review

Review reports, complaints, disclosure quality, and local compliance assumptions before scaling.

Day 29

Go/no-go decision

Compare metrics against kill criteria. Decide whether to continue, narrow wedge, or stop.

Day 30

Next sprint memo

Write the build plan for the next 30 days: automate ingestion, improve catalog, add reservations, or expand supply.

Decision gate

Proceed only if at least 30% of buyers open the digest 3+ times/week, 20% click/message a listing weekly, 10+ sellers post twice, and there are no unresolved trust/compliance blockers.
